# OBAG: Open borders in the current political climate

Post link: https://www.facebook.com/groups/openborders/posts/25401081622895219/

## Post

In the past few years, discussion in this group, as well as new posts on openborders.info, have died down quite a bit. A lot of this is because the individuals who were blogging most regularly on the site (Nathan, John, myself, Michelangelo) switched focus to other things, for diverse reasons of personal circumstance.

But I think there has also been a general vibe shift away from the style of writing and discussion that we used to have on the site and in this Facebook group, of polite, thoughtful discussion of radical improvements, and more toward fighting to defend the status quo from attacks coming from other directions.

For instance, sites like https://www.theunpopulist.net/ and https://liberalcurrents.com/ (both of which I personally like quite a bit and read often) are currently quite active and high-profile sites that attract many of the same sorts of people who would in the past have contributed to openborders.info (and there's in some cases literal overlap, with people who had blogged or guest-blogged for openborders.info or contributed to OBAG writing content for these sites). But they have a very different flavor from openborders.info, including such things as:

* Greater focus on defending the core tenets of (democratic) classical liberalism, a kind of "least common denominator" of a big tent that might include moderate progressives, libertarians, liberaltarians (not sure if that is a thing any more, but the general idea holds), neoliberals, and anti-MAGA conservatives, and for the most part focusing on ideas that would be agreeable within this broad big tent.

* Much more focus on current events, including in many cases explicit advocacy of how to respond to them and a tone highlighting how important it is to focus on them. While at openborders.info, we often talked about current events, these were often used as starting points for broader discussions rather than endpoints to rally around.

I don't think anybody has said it explicitly, but I think there's an implicit view embedded in the general shift in people's focus. Namely, that in the current moment, with imminent threats around the globe to basic tenets that undergird free societies, it's a waste of resources to focus on quixotic and pie-in-the-sky visions of radical improvement. These resources should instead be marshaled into defending the status quo. In the language of fights for ideological territory, the goal should be to defend one's ground rather than advance into new territory.

Is this an uncharitable reading of the general vibe shift?

## Reply to John Lee's reply

Great points! I see your three points as being about production (the quality or existence value of the content produced), consumption (the ease of consuming it), and distribution/reach (how effectively the content reaches a large enough audience of the desired characteristics).

Here are some thoughts on the production angle specifically in the context of comparing long-form text posts and long-form (multi-person) podcasts (the ascendant format these days).

[For context, I consume a fair amount of podcasts, and I'd say most of the ones I consume are information-dense and are moderately popular (i.e., these are not super-niche stuff). Some of the general categories with a few leading examples:

* Stuff around understanding society, technology, and the future: 80,000 Hours and Dwarkesh Patel
* Stuff around health and longevity: Peter Attia, Matt Kaeberlein, and Simon Hill

While I have listened to segments of Joe Rogan podcasts (mostly to get a better sense of the source discussion for stuff that ended up getting discussed or reacted upon elsewhere) I consider Rogan a "softball interviewer". My general impression is that guests go on Rogan primarily for the distribution, and primarily to make themselves appear more human than the caricatures of them that might exist. Not that there's anything wrong with that, but it's not really what I focus on when thinking about the best way to do a podcast for the sort of goals I have in mind.]

There are two features on the production side of (multi-person) podcasts that make them different from long-form text content: (1) short turns, and (2) rapid responses. The consumption side -- the lack of links and cross-references (both internal and external) -- also influences the production side somewhat, though many of the best podcasts include show notes with the links and cross-references needed.

The "short turns" piece is that every individual turn (one person speaking, mostly uninterrupted other than acks) is generally only a few minutes (Claude says 3-7 minutes for complex podcasts, though my personal experience is generally that it's rarely more than 5 minutes even for the most complex podcasts). This has both plus and minuses. It's good that it means no individual person can go on a super-long tangent -- the other person brings it down to earth and seeks clarification. It's bad because sometimes you need a longer stretch of time to build a complex argument, and have people follow you on faith, and that's not the sort of thing the podcast format is generally for.

The "rapid responses" piece is the amazingly little time people have to craft responses. For instance, compare with this OBAG post. The length of responses here is comparable with the length of answers in a podcast. But, for instance, I took about 20 minutes to draft the original post after mulling it overnight. I mulled comments in the back of my mind before responding to them, and crafting the response often took more than twice the time it would take to say the response out loud after written down. (This particular comment took me about 20 minutes to draft, after mulling it over for at least an hour while doing other stuff).

In the softball interviewer world, the rapid responses piece leads to low information density and generally mundane conversation. But there are a bunch of highly information-dense podcasts despite the rapid responses piece. How does this work? Fundamentally, I think this is a function of the selection of topics and the podcast interviewees. The most information-dense podcasts tend to be interviews of people who have years of expertise and experience working in and thinking about the topic. In many cases, the interviewer, while perhaps not as well-versed, has also spent years engaged with the topic. This means that they often have cached responses in their head to a wide range of questions (Some prior prep / coordination before the podcast also helps).

But this need for rapid responses makes it fundamentally challenging to adapt the podcast format to topics that are inherently exploratory/speculative in nature where the people involved are not experts on the subject. In contrast, when writing a blog post, even the non-expert who is moderately informed can do research over several hours and put something together. If I look back on several of the good posts on openborders.info, many of them were on subjects that we were not experts at, but knew enough to be able to research and think through over several hours, and then link to the experts within our posts. This allowed for a larger breadth of coverage.

These considerations make predictions that mirror my experience: the information-dense value-laden podcast seems to work when interviewing people to unpack some of their rich domain expertise, with an interviewer who is skilled enough to ask the questions. But it is not that much of a success when it comes to trying to extempore one's way into inherently exploratory/speculative stuff (which is almost by definition the case for a lot of discussion of open borders, given how far that is from the current reality), except when it has been extensively prepped (in which case it loses some of the spontaneity of a podcast and becomes closer to a read-aloud rehearsed interaction, like a transcript of this OBAG post and its comments).

In light of all this, what kind of podcast-like stuff might make sense? I think it would make sense more as a followup to unpack stuff we've already written. For instance, Nathan has written extensively on how the economy and society might evolve under open borders. After reviewing all of his own work, I feel confident Nathan could have an engaging, information-dense real-time exchange in a podcast format with somebody who has also read his work, and may have various points of disagreement or curiosity. But, I would not expect such a podcast to be useful if it had happened before Nathan had already written his posts (or even to be valuable if he or the interviewer had not recently reviewed his writing on the subject).
